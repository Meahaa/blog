Integrating Interfaces

A personal exploration into understanding the dos and donts of integrated physical-digital interface design.

The Problem

With the advancement of technology, many consumer products are beginning to harness the power of using both digital and physical interfaces. The integration of interactions design and industrial design can result in richer, deeper experiences. Unfortunately, when executed incorrectly, this integration can lead to confusion or even failure of product usability. It’s a major design challenge to determine how to define the boundary between physical and digital interfaces and which medium each task/scenario should employ. In many products, this combination is used ineffectively, or may not be necessary at all. In fact, it’s not uncommon to see designs using known physical or digital interface design guidelines improperly.

IoT devices rely heavily on the interface between the physical and digital world, this could be why it has become so difficult to integrate many of these devices into our lives. These take many items of processes in the home and have added a digitized, controllable component (typically from a mobile or desktop interface). Taking physical objects that have been designed and refined over centuries, and adding an additional layer of technology to provide a wider set of functionality.

Skeuomorphism

Skeuomorphism is a design methodology developed in the 80s in which an object in software mimics its real-life counterpart. This is a method that clearly harnesses the joint power of two design fields, where interaction design takes inspiration from industrial design to create digital affordances. Material design methodology uses many skeuomorphic elements in the design language. Skeuomorphism was extremely valuable in introducing digital usability patterns when digital interfaces were first released. Common examples are:

  • Shadows around elements which are intended to be clickable
  • The trashcan to indicate where deletion of files

Although in the past, skeuomorphism had been slowly phased out and replaced with flat design methodologies, it has recently begun to regain popularity with the release of new and innovative technology. Internet of Things devices and wearables all seem to some degree, apply skeuomorphic techniques to their designs. The Nest Thermostat and the Apple Watch are good examples of skeuomorphic design being applied to help integrate physical and digital controls and products.

Benefits of Integrated Interfaces
  1. Having digital and industrial design work together can create a unified digital-physical brand experience that acts across all interfaces is most impactful and memorable.
  2. Harnessing the knowledge and experience from two industries to create a richer, more fluid user experience.
  3. Historically, no further improvements can be made without significant retooling to a physical product after it’s released, but incorporating digital elements and the ability to update firmware allows for post-launch updates to be pushed out to customers. Interactive onboarding, introducing new features, and responding to customer feedback lead to increased customer engagement and brand advocacy.
Negatives of Integrated Products
  1. Using the real world to augment user experiences can result in clunky user experiences that bounce users. A well-designed physical product and a beautiful, usable digital interface can quickly fail people when either the physical or digital aspects have been developed without considering the other.
  2. Unnecessary additions: With the rapid development of technology, it sometimes seems as if digital interfaces may just be integrated since the technologically is available. This leads to the creation of products that do not have an integrated experience, but rather two separate experiences competing with each other.
My Recommendations

Because the functionality of each integrated product is so unique, it is difficult to develop a set of design standards to help designers create amazing joint physical and digital interfaces. Instead, I believe a set of recommendations on the approach to such problems, is more beneficial. These are as follows.

  • Research: It is imperative that design research into both the physical and cognitive tolerances in context of the user’s environment is conducted.
  • Cross Functional Teams: Diverse teams are critical to designing physical and digital experiences. Ensure that it is created together, not as siloed opportunities.
  • Affordances: Create affordances for both physical and digital interfaces, in their correct mode. Ensure that the affordances indicate which interface the user should interact with.
  • Feedback: Provide valuable feedback on system status of both the physical and digital interfaces.
  • Models: With the complexity of another layer in the interface mode, it is extremely important to align conceptual model with user’s mental model.
  • Workflow Analysis: Do not add extra, unnecessary steps just to harness the power of both interfaces: ie. adding an app to turn on your lights: 1. Switch, 2. Pull out phone, unlock phone, open app, turn on lights
